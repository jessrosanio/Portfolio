Overview

This site serves as a resource for anyone interested in delving into the architectural legacy of Harrisonburg, Virginia. Through a meticulously curated collection of photographs, detailed descriptions, and historical accounts, this website offers an exploration of the city's significant historic buildings that are demolished or in the process of restoration. Each structure holds a unique story, reflecting the diverse eras and influences that have shaped Harrisonburg's identity. Whether you are a history enthusiast, a student, or a curious visitor, this website invites you to appreciate and understand the buildings that contribute to the city's cultural heritage. Discover the stories behind these buildings and gain a deeper understanding of the controversy behind demolishing these historic buildings of Harrisonburg.

Visit the site at: https://jessrosanio.github.io/Harrisonburg-Historic-Buildings/index.html

Background

The Harrisonburg Historic Buildings website is the result of extensive research conducted by students of a research class within the Media Arts and Design major. With a meticulous approach, these researchers delved into various primary and secondary sources, including historical records, archival materials, books, photographs, and interviews. This comprehensive process aimed to gather accurate and reliable information about each historic building featured on the website. This class also collaborated with local historians, preservation organizations, and community members to obtain valuable insights and stories related to the buildings. Through this collaborative research, the website offers a portrayal of Harrisonburg's architectural history utilizing the information provided by SMAD researchers. It strives to provide a platform where historical accuracy meets engaging narratives, enabling visitors to explore and appreciate the significance of these buildings within the broader context of Harrisonburg's heritage.

Researched Buildings

"Each time we lose a historic building in Harrisonburg, community members get angry, they lament the loss, and then this cycle repeats when another historic building is demolished. This keeps happening because we don’t have a local historic district with controls in place to protect our irreplaceable buildings that embody our history.” - Downtown Historic Renaissance
Haas House - Demolished

The Haas House was demolished last spring by JMU and was the oldest building left on South Main Street.

Dallard-Newman House - Preserved

The Dallard-Newman House is one of the only surviving historic African American homes in the Valley, 137 years later becoming a museum.

The Foundry - Still Standing

Today, P. Bradley and Sons has transformed into a local farm equipment store as the need for these products increased over the years.

Shoppes at Mauzy - Demolished

The Shoppes at Mauzy was demolished around August in 2022 and is under construction to become a Dollar General.
